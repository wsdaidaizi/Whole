# Mainland China — CFPS

## Scope

The China component uses linked parent–child observations from the China Family Panel Studies (CFPS). It covers two directly estimated attitude domains:

- local-government evaluation;
- gender-role attitudes, coded so that higher values are more traditional.

The estimates are conditional associations, not identified causal effects of parents on children.

## Baseline specification

\[
Y_{iwp}=\alpha+\beta P_{iwp}+\Gamma X_{iwp}+\delta_w+\phi_p+\varepsilon_{iwp}
\]

Controls include child age and gender, parents’ mean age and education, household income, household assets, urban residence, self/proxy-report indicators, wave fixed effects, and province fixed effects. Standard errors are clustered at the child level.

## Core results

| Outcome | Parent coefficient | Clustered SE | Observations | Children |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Local-government evaluation | 0.164 | 0.010 | 14,458 | 8,821 |
| Gender-role attitudes | 0.170 | 0.015 | 9,426 | 7,848 |

Both domains show positive adjusted parent–child resemblance.

## Gender heterogeneity

Formal pooled interaction models do not detect stable differences between mothers and fathers, daughters and sons, or same-gender and mixed-gender dyads. Numerical subgroup differences should therefore not be interpreted as evidence that one parent or child gender transmits attitudes more strongly.

## Contextual moderation

Provincial GDP per capita, secondary-industry share, urban context, and household assets do not produce a general, multiplicity-robust pattern of weaker parent–child resemblance. The China evidence therefore does not support a simple modernization-as-family-substitution claim.

## Interpretation

The strongest defensible conclusion is broad adjusted resemblance in both political evaluation and gender-role norms. Shared institutions, media environments, residence, family history, measurement error, and unobserved characteristics remain possible explanations.
