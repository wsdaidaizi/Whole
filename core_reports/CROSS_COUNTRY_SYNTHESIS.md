# Cross-National Synthesis

## Comparative question

The project asks whether parent–child attitude resemblance persists across societies with different levels of economic development, institutional density, urbanization, and family organization, and whether contextual change weakens or reshapes that resemblance.

## Finding 1 — Parent–child resemblance is widely replicated

Positive conditional associations appear across political evaluation, gender/family norms, religion, subjective economic position, social participation, general values, voting, party orientation, trust, and family traditionalism. The pattern is not confined to one country group or one attitude domain.

This is strongest as a statement about **resemblance**, not causal transmission. The studies do not isolate parental influence from shared environments, assortative residence, common institutions, reverse influence, or unobserved family characteristics.

## Finding 2 — Resemblance is strongly domain-specific

The same dataset can show clear resemblance in one domain and little in another. Indonesia, for example, shows positive associations in religiosity, subjective welfare, and arisan participation but near-zero risk/time-preference associations. The UK displays positive associations across many domains, but their magnitudes depend heavily on scale construction and denominator. Cross-domain coefficients therefore should not be ranked as if they measured one universal latent trait.

## Finding 3 — Modernization does not simply erase the family

The evidence does not support a universal substitution model in which development, urbanization, education, or institutions consistently reduce the parent–child slope.

- China: provincial GDP, urban context, industry share, and household assets do not yield a general multiplicity-robust weakening pattern.
- Mexico: municipality manufacturing interactions are not supported in pooled individual-level models.
- Taiwan: none of the large prespecified contextual-interaction family survives BH-FDR.
- DHS: geographic, shock, and capacity interactions are localized and proxy-dependent rather than a stable cross-country mechanism.
- UK: family resemblance remains broad in a high-income welfare-state setting.

A better summary is that economic and institutional context often has **additive effects on children’s attitudes** and may selectively alter particular domains, but does not uniformly dissolve intergenerational resemblance.

## Finding 4 — Apparent geographic differences require shrinkage and direct tests

Local and country-specific coefficients differ, but raw rankings are unreliable when sample sizes, cluster counts, ceiling concentration, and attitude variation differ. Formal interaction tests, coefficient contrasts, hierarchical partial pooling, and multiple-testing adjustment are required. A significant coefficient in one place and a nonsignificant coefficient in another is not itself evidence that the places differ.

## Finding 5 — Measurement and estimand differences are central

Cross-country comparisons must harmonize:

- item wording and scale direction;
- parent–child timing;
- age and coresidence restrictions;
- covariates and fixed effects;
- clustering and weights;
- binary-outcome denominators;
- multiple-testing families.

The current project is best organized by attitude domain and design rather than as a simple country ranking.

## Manuscript-ready conclusion

Across less-developed, developing, and developed settings, families remain sites of substantial attitude resemblance. Modernization does not produce a uniform decline in the parent–child association. Instead, resemblance is domain-specific and embedded in different institutional, geographic, and measurement environments. Contextual moderators occasionally matter, but the evidence is selective and rarely survives a common multiplicity standard. The project therefore supports a model of **persistent but reconfigured family resemblance**, not straightforward family disappearance.
